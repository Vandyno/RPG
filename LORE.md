# World Summary

Velcor is a dark storybook fantasy setting built for a tile-based open-world
RPG. It should feel eerie, old, atmospheric, strange, and culturally layered
without making the player-facing mechanics hard to understand.

The setting should support Skyrim-like worldbuilding through regions, peoples,
religions, cults, political tensions, towns, hubs, local conflicts, readables,
and dangerous roads.

Design principle:

High-imagination world, low-friction gameplay.

The weirdness should mostly live in:
- areas
- lore
- peoples
- religions
- cults
- faction conflict
- rare supernatural conditions
- item descriptions
- quests

Combat should stay simple and readable:
- melee attacks
- ranged attacks
- fireballs
- shields
- healing
- basic spells
- clear enemies
- clear encounter structure

The earlier silver orchard idea is not currently a setting pillar. It may become
one optional location later, or it may be removed.

---

# Tone

The tone is dark storybook fantasy: grounded, eerie, symbolic, and old. The
world should have room for grief, faith, superstition, strange rituals, local
politics, humour, cults, and quiet dread.

The world should avoid generic fantasy filler. Locations should feel like they
exist because of history, need, faith, trade, fear, or old bargains.

# Major Peoples

## Humans

Humans are the baseline people of Velcor.

They occupy villages, towns, cities, farms, mage colleges, chapels, roadside
inns, and old kingdoms. Humans are the most religiously diverse and politically
divided people.

Humans are expanding slowly. They need timber, farmland, roads, and settlements,
but they cannot freely clear forests without risking conflict with the Rootborn.

To avoid war, many human regions rely on:
- managed groves
- designated lumber forests
- replanting laws
- forbidden old-growth zones
- negotiated cutting rights
- Rootborn observers or wardens

Most humans do not want war with the Rootborn.

Core tension:

Humans need to grow. Rootborn need old places respected.

## Tanglekin

Working name: Tanglekin.

Agile, clever, physical people. They should feel adventurous and mythic without
becoming comic relief.

Common roles:
- climbers
- tricksters
- monks
- duelists
- messengers
- thieves
- performers
- travellers

Their culture values:
- motion
- wit
- learning from failure
- teachers
- stories passed through action
- self-overcoming

## Tuskfolk

Working name: Tuskfolk.

Orc-like people, but not generic evil raiders. They are strong, practical,
tusked people associated with road clans, smithing, forts, mercenary work,
oath-keeping, endurance, and old military routes.

They can be allies, rivals, soldiers, merchants, smiths, guards, or enemies
depending on circumstance.

## Ravenfolk

Ravenfolk are rare and mysterious. They should not be common townspeople.
Seeing one should feel meaningful, like an omen or an important event.

Associated with:
- knowledge
- death
- messages
- secrets
- records
- omens
- old archives

Use them sparingly to preserve their mystery.

## Rootborn

Rootborn are plant and nature-linked people associated with roots, soil, shade,
memory, patience, forests, seasons, and old natural places.

They are not simply elves. They should feel older, stranger, more rooted, and
more tied to place.

Their conflict with humans is one of the major grounded tensions of the setting:
human expansion versus respect for old growth and sacred natural places.

# Religions And Beliefs

Religion should work like culture, politics, identity, and daily practice, not
just a list of gods.

## The Sainted Church

The dominant organized human religion in many towns and cities.

The Sainted Church venerates:
- dead saints
- martyrs
- protectors
- old heroes
- relics
- burial rites
- confession
- penance

Common imagery:
- candles
- bells
- relics
- graves
- chapels
- stone saints

Values:
- order
- duty
- guilt
- penance
- protection of the dead
- moral consequence
- memory

Power base:
- cities
- courts
- chapels
- graveyards
- old towns
- some noble houses

Risk:

The Church can become authoritarian, suspicious, and politically entangled.

## The Old Road Faith

An older rural human belief practiced by farmers, travellers, hunters,
innkeepers, and roadside villages.

It involves offerings to:
- local powers
- road spirits
- nameless guardians
- harvest forces
- old taboos

Values:
- hospitality
- safe travel
- luck
- harvest
- weather
- omens
- bargains

The Sainted Church often tolerates it publicly but may dismiss it as
superstition.

## The Star Measure

A mage-college and scholar religion-philosophy. It is not exactly worship. It
treats reality as something ordered by hidden laws that can be studied, named,
measured, and manipulated.

Values:
- knowledge
- discipline
- magical theory
- self-mastery
- study of reality's hidden structure

Power base:
- mage colleges
- scholars
- some nobles
- magical institutions

The Sainted Church may see it as arrogant or spiritually dangerous.

## The Thousand Steps

Tanglekin religion. A path-based faith built around motion, cleverness,
teachers, ancestors, and self-overcoming.

Core belief:

Life is a climb. Every mistake is a branch. Every escape is a lesson. Every
story is a handhold.

Values:
- agility
- wit
- humility after failure
- loyalty to teachers
- sacred storytelling
- self-improvement through movement and experience

Humans may mistake it for unseriousness, but it has real discipline.

## The Oathfire

Tuskfolk religion. A forge-and-ancestor faith.

To the Tuskfolk, words have weight. A broken oath stains the soul; a kept oath
strengthens the dead who came before.

Values:
- oaths
- craft
- endurance
- family lines
- honour
- practical courage
- ancestors

Holy places:
- forges
- cairns
- old battle roads
- ancestral halls

Tuskfolk may respect the seriousness of the Sainted Church but dislike
confession without action.

## The Last Archive

Ravenfolk religion. Rare, private, and mysterious.

Ravenfolk believe every life must be witnessed, remembered, and eventually
carried into the final record.

Values:
- memory
- secrets
- death
- messages
- truth
- endings
- records

Holy places:
- hidden archives
- black towers
- battlefield trees
- sealed libraries

Everyone respects them, but almost no one fully trusts them.

## The Deep Root

Rootborn religion. A nature-memory faith.

The Rootborn believe life is held by soil, shade, ancestry, seasons, and place.
To be healthy is to be rooted. To be lost is to be cut from what remembers you.

Values:
- patience
- memory
- shade
- balance
- burial
- old forests
- continuity
- respect for place

Holy places:
- groves
- ancient trees
- root-halls
- sacred springs
- forbidden old-growth regions

They clash with uncontrolled logging, mining, road-building, and human
expansion.

# Shadow Wane

The Shadow Wane is not a race. It is a rare condition, curse, or affliction.

A person's shadow gradually weakens instead of disappearing all at once. This
should be uncommon, but possible in the world.

Symbolism:

The shadow represents consequence: the weight of a person's actions following
them.

As a shadow wanes, the person does not instantly become evil or insane. Instead,
they slowly lose emotional connection to:
- guilt
- shame
- fear
- consequence

They may still understand morality intellectually, but it starts to feel distant
or unreal.

Possible signs:
- the shadow looks too pale
- it lags slightly behind
- it shrinks at the wrong time
- it trembles when the person lies
- it fails to match the body correctly
- it vanishes in candlelight
- it splits at the edges

Stages:

1. Full Shadow: normal, grounded person.
2. Pale Cast: the shadow is faint; guilt, fear, love, and shame still exist,
   but pass too quickly.
3. Thin Shadow: the shadow moves slightly wrong; consequences stop sticking
   emotionally.
4. Half-Wane: the shadow is broken or patchy; morality begins to feel abstract.
5. Last Cast: only a small shadow remains. The person may desperately try to
   hold on through oaths, journals, rituals, friends, Rootborn help, painted
   shadows, repeated confessions, or personal rules.
6. Shadowless: the shadow is gone. The person may remain calm and functional,
   but is no longer properly tethered to consequence.

Usage rule:

The Shadow Wane should be a rare setting detail, not the main explanation for
the whole world. Most villains, bandits, and cruel people are still ordinary
people. Occasionally, however, someone's shadow is wrong, and that adds
supernatural unease.

# Cults

Cults exist across cultures. They should not be limited to one race or people.

## The Ashen Choir

Sainted Church extremists.

A radical splinter cult that believes the world is corrupt because people have
become too tolerant of old faiths, nonhuman customs, cults, and "soft heresies."

They want purification through:
- confession
- fire
- relic law
- forced penance
- saintly violence

They are not officially the Church, but some priests may secretly support them.

Theme:

Religious extremism, purification, control, fear of corruption.

## The Noonday Cult

A cult obsessed with removing shadows and escaping consequence.

They believe guilt, shame, fear, and consequence are chains. They gather at
noon, when shadows are smallest.

Slogan:

No shadow, no burden.

They may seek to induce, exploit, or worship the Shadow Wane.

Theme:

Freedom from consequence, moral numbness, dangerous liberation.

## Hollow Mercy

A drug-related cult or criminal-religious movement.

They distribute a substance that dulls:
- pain
- guilt
- fear
- memory

Possible names for the drug:
- Mercy
- White Mercy
- Hush
- Saint's Milk
- Pale Sap
- Dream Ash

Core pitch:

The world is cruel. We offer mercy.

The drug begins as relief:
- grief fades
- pain dulls
- fear softens
- guilt quiets
- yesterday stops hurting

Long-term use leaves people:
- emotionally hollow
- detached
- dependent
- suggestible

In rare cases, it may contribute to Shadow Wane.

They prey on grieving families, wounded soldiers, failed students, overworked
labourers, refugees, criminals, prisoners, and people in plague towns.

Faction reactions:
- The Sainted Church publicly condemns them, though corrupt clergy may secretly
  use or sell the drug.
- Rootborn hate them if the drug is made from stolen sap, roots, or sacred
  plants.
- Tuskfolk see it as oath-rot and weakness, though veterans may fall into it.
- Tanglekin smugglers may move it through cities and roads.
- Ravenfolk quietly track where it appears.

Theme:

Addiction, grief, exploitation, relief, numbness, supernatural risk.

# Relations Between Peoples

## Humans And Rootborn

Major tension.

Humans need timber, farms, settlements, roads, and survival. Rootborn need
forests, old growth, sacred natural places, slow continuity, and protected
memory of place.

Most humans do not want war. The result is controlled tree-farming, negotiated
borders, forbidden forests, periodic local disputes, Rootborn observation of
human cutting rights, and fragile treaties.

## Humans And Tuskfolk

Tense but practical. They trade, fight together, hire each other, and sometimes
remember old wars.

Mutual respect is possible, especially around discipline, duty, craft, and
endurance.

## Humans And Tanglekin

Common contact.

Humans may stereotype Tanglekin as thieves, performers, wanderers, or unreliable
tricksters. In reality, Tanglekin culture has discipline and deep tradition.

## Humans And Ravenfolk

Reverence mixed with fear.

Ravenfolk are too rare to be treated normally. A Ravenfolk arrival often creates
anxiety, rumours, political attention, and religious interpretation.

## Tuskfolk And Rootborn

Potential mutual respect. Both value endurance, old ties, continuity, and long
memory.

Conflict can arise over roads, mines, forts, and borders.

## Tuskfolk And Tanglekin

Rivalry.

Tuskfolk value oaths, directness, and endurance. Tanglekin value movement, wit,
and flexibility. They can clash, but also complement each other.

## Tanglekin And Ravenfolk

Both value knowledge, but differently.

Tanglekin prefer living stories, oral tradition, movement, and lessons learned
through action. Ravenfolk prefer fixed records, witnessed truths, and final
accounts.

## Rootborn And Ravenfolk

Quiet respect. Both care about memory, death, continuity, and what must not be
forgotten.

# Regions And Locations

## Human Heartland

### The Marches Of Velcor

The main human heartland.

Fertile, road-bound, politically important, and relatively stable compared to
the outer regions. It is the center of human settlement, trade, church
influence, and slow expansion.

### Cairnwall

The oldest major human city on the map and a stronghold of the Sainted Church.

Known for cathedrals, graveyards, relics, old stone walls, and religious
authority. It is pious, influential, and not always welcoming.

### Dunmere

The busiest trade city in the region.

Built around river movement, commerce, crossroads traffic, and political
bargaining. Rich, practical, crowded, and one of the easiest places for cults,
smugglers, and political interests to hide.

### Hallowrest

A pilgrimage town with shrines, burial grounds, mourning rites, and quiet
religious authority. People come here for mourning, healing, remembrance, and
burial rites.

### Briarwatch

A frontier town near Rootborn lands.

Built around negotiation, caution, controlled expansion, lumber treaties,
patrols, and local tensions.

### Northgate

A modest northern town that serves as a road junction and local administrative
point. Important mainly because it sits between safer human lands and the more
uncertain northern boundary.

### Stonebridge

A crossing town built around a reliable river passage. Practical, sturdy, and
important for movement of goods and patrols.

### Greenford

A rural trade settlement with good farmland and road access. It supplies food,
timber products, and traffic support to larger settlements.

### Valeham

A quiet inland town tied to agriculture and local chapels. More traditional and
slower-moving than the cities.

### Wensford

A western road and coastal access town. Useful for fishing, local trade, and
sea-adjacent travel.

### Redfield

A farming settlement with decent land but some unease around it. Small enough to
be overlooked, which makes it useful for smugglers or hidden cult influence.

### Oakholt

A wooded village on the edge of managed forest territory. Known for timber work,
replanting, and local tensions over what may or may not be cut.

### Mirthbridge

A smaller settlement near Dunmere that benefits from river traffic and overflow
commerce. Less glamorous, more practical.

### Fairmead

An agricultural town in the eastern stretches of the heartland. Stable,
productive, and tied to grain and livestock.

### Southwatch

A harsher southern outpost near rougher country. More defensive than prosperous.

## Rootborn Lands

### The Elderweald

The oldest and most imposing forest in the region.

Deep, ancient, and unmistakably separate from ordinary woodland. This is not
just a forest. It feels like a place with memory.

### Mosshollow

A Rootborn gathering place hidden within the Elderweald.

Not a bustling city, but a living center of counsel, ritual, continuity, and
Rootborn memory.

### The Shadegroves

Sacred Rootborn woodland territory. More protected, more openly spiritual, and
more guarded than ordinary forest. Outsiders are not welcome without reason.

### The Greenwood Boundary

The line between human-managed lands and Rootborn-protected territory.

Part treaty line, part warning. It represents peace, compromise, old agreements,
and constant risk of failure.

## Tanglekin Lands

### The Tangle Heights

A rugged highland region of cliffs, narrow paths, elevated settlements, and hard
travel. Strongly shaped by Tanglekin culture.

### Stepcliff Monastery

A major spiritual and cultural site of the Thousand Steps.

Known for discipline, instruction, severe terrain, training, reflection, and
endurance.

### Branchrest

A Tanglekin trade hub built in difficult country. It connects highland routes
and traveling peoples. It should feel more agile and improvised than human
towns.

## Ravenfolk Sites

### Blackrook Archive

A remote Ravenfolk record-house.

A place of old records, witnessed truths, sealed knowledge, and guarded memory.
It should feel important and difficult to access.

### The Last Perch

A hidden Ravenfolk sanctuary or watch-site. Sparse, elevated, and mysterious.
The kind of place people speak of more than they see.

## Tuskfolk Lands

### The Iron March

A harsh frontier region shaped by endurance, fortification, old obligations,
hard travel, and Tuskfolk identity.

Less fertile, more severe, and deeply tied to Tuskfolk culture.

### Karag-Tor

A major Tuskfolk stronghold. Militarily important, physically imposing, and
culturally significant.

A place of authority, oaths, defense, and Tuskfolk power.

### Ashforge Hold

A smithing center and stronghold tied to the Oathfire. Known for metalwork,
discipline, ancestral craft, and oathbound warriors.

### The Oathroad

A fortified route connecting Tuskfolk lands and holds.

More than a road. It is a symbol of old duty, maintained passage, iron
commitments, and military history.

## Major Natural And Frontier Regions

### Hollow Coast

A rugged western coastline with rough rock, cold water, and exposed settlements.
Atmospheric, isolated, and dangerous in poor weather.

### Lake Orin

A large inland lake that gives the map balance and trade relevance. Calm in
appearance, but important politically and geographically.

### The Blackwater

A darker, more forbidding water region. Remote, severe, and not associated with
comfort.

### The Greyfang Range

A cold, ancient mountain barrier. Dangerous, difficult to cross, and visually
dominant. A strong natural divide between regions.

### The Red Fields

The agricultural breadbasket of Velcor. Broad, productive, and essential to the
human heartland.

### The Mireveil

A swamp region of mist, bad footing, and drowned paths.

Good for isolation, secrets, strange creatures, smugglers, and cult activity.

### The Weeping Vale

A sorrowful valley region with a heavier emotional tone. It feels like a place
people pass through carefully, not a place they settle without reason.

### The Silent Barrows

An old burial region of tombs and dead memory.

A place tied to forgotten dead, old fears, superstition, and possible ancient
history.

# Current Conflicts

- Human expansion versus Rootborn protection of old forests.
- Sainted Church authority versus old local faiths and scholar-magic.
- Hollow Mercy exploiting grief, pain, addiction, and numbness.
- The Ashen Choir trying to purify "soft heresies" through coercion.
- The Noonday Cult seeking freedom from consequence through shadowlessness.
- Trade roads, frontier towns, and border settlements becoming pressure points
  for cultural and political conflict.

- Do the Rootborn remember things humans have intentionally forgotten?
- What records does Blackrook Archive refuse to open?
- Is Hollow Mercy only a drug, or also a ritual substance?
- Which priests quietly support the Ashen Choir?
- What does the Noonday Cult know that ordinary scholars do not?

# First Vertical Slice Canon Candidates

Good first-slice regions:
- Briarwatch: human/Rootborn tension, logging treaty, local frontier trouble.
- Redfield: farming town with hidden Hollow Mercy influence.
- Oakholt: managed forest village with replanting law and cutting disputes.
- Stonebridge: practical road/crossing hub with bandit or cult movement.

Good first-slice quest shape:
- local dispute
- one quest-giver
- one readable clue
- one small hostile encounter
- one item pickup
- one moral or faction consequence
- one world-state flag set by reading
- one world-state flag set by quest completion

Example seed:

A managed grove has been cut beyond its treaty line near Briarwatch. Humans and
Rootborn accuse each other of lying. The player investigates moved boundary
stones and discovers Hollow Mercy smugglers using the dispute as cover.

# Canon Rules

- Generated quest ideas are proposals, not canon.
- Do not make Shadow Wane the explanation for every villain or conflict.
- Do not make any people innately evil.
- Keep combat and immediate gameplay readable.
- Let lore and consequences add depth without overcomplicating the first slice.
- Prefer local problems that connect to larger tensions.
- Use stable IDs from `CONTENT_GUIDE.md` when turning lore into data.
